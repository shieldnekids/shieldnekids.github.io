Why We Do This

Every single day, predators are sliding into kids' DMs, grooming them, and setting up meetings. Law enforcement is stretched thin. Cases get dropped. Predators walk. The system fails kids more often than it protects them.

We're not waiting for the system to catch up. We're building a team that identifies these people, gathers rock-solid evidence, works with law enforcement when they'll act, and exposes predators publicly when they won't. Every predator we catch is one less person who gets to hurt a child.

This document lays out everything — the legal framework across four New England states, how we build our team, how we work with ICAC, how we handle evidence, and how we escalate when the system doesn't do its job. We've done the homework. Now we execute.

The Escalation Model

We're not going in guns blazing on day one. We follow a process. ICAC and law enforcement get first shot. But when the system fails — and it will sometimes — we escalate. Here's how it works:

1

Gather Evidence

Decoy operation, chat logs, screenshots, screen recordings. Build an airtight case.

2

ICAC / Law Enforcement

Hand everything over. Give them a real chance to act. Follow up. Push for charges.

3

Public Exposure

If charges don't happen or get dropped — the video goes up. Their face, their words, their intent. The internet doesn't forget.

4

Live Confrontation

Face-to-face. On camera. If things get physical, we accept what comes with that. Predators should never feel safe.

Why ICAC Gets First Shot

ICAC might already be running a long investigation on the same person. If we blow that up with a premature confrontation, we could tank months of their work and let a predator walk on a technicality. Reaching out to ICAC first costs us nothing but a phone call. If they're already on it, we stay out of the way and let the pros finish. If they're not, we have a contact who knows our work and might pick up the case. Either way, we win.

The confrontation and public exposure are always on the table. They're the escalation when the system doesn't deliver. But ICAC gets the first look every time. Out of courtesy to the people who eat, sleep, and breathe this nightmare daily — we give them the shot before we go public.

ICAC Integration Playbook

ICAC (Internet Crimes Against Children) task forces are the cops who actually specialize in this. There are 61 task forces nationwide with over 5,400 agencies. Every state we're operating in has one. Building a relationship with them is the single biggest force multiplier we have.

Step 1: Make First Contact

Before we run a single operation, we reach out to the ICAC task forces in all four states. Here's how:

  • Find your local ICAC — icactaskforce.org lists every task force by state
  • Call the non-emergency line for your local PD or state police and ask to speak with someone from the ICAC unit or Internet Crimes division
  • Introduce the organization — who we are, what our mission is, and that we want to work WITH them, not around them
  • Explain our methods — decoy profiles, chat evidence collection, how we preserve evidence, and that we operate independently (important for the entrapment issue)
  • Ask for a point of contact — a specific detective or agent we can reach when we have something
  • Ask if they have any guidance — they may have specific things they want or don't want us to do

Some of them will be receptive, some won't. That's fine. We document every contact attempt and keep trying. The ones who work with us become our most valuable relationships.

Step 2: The Evidence Handoff Protocol

Every time we build a case, ICAC gets the evidence packet before anything goes public. Here's the exact protocol:

  1. Complete the investigation — full chat logs, screen recordings, all evidence preserved with SHA-256 hashes
  2. Prepare the packet — printed chat logs, USB drive with all digital evidence, written timeline, suspect info, decoy profile details
  3. Contact our ICAC point person — "We have a case, here's a brief summary, when can we bring you the evidence?"
  4. Hand it off in person — file a formal report at the station in the jurisdiction where the suspect traveled to (or would have traveled to)
  5. Get a case number — this is critical for tracking
  6. Ask the key question: "Is this person already on your radar or part of an ongoing investigation?" — if yes, we back off completely and let them work
  7. Set a follow-up date — "I'll check back in two weeks for an update"

Step 3: The Follow-Up Cadence

TimelineAction

We give the system a real, honest chance to work. Two months. If nothing happens after we've done everything right and handed them a case on a silver platter — that's when the video goes up and the community finds out who this person is.

Step 4: Building the Ongoing Relationship

  • Be professional every single time. We're not calling to complain. We're partners, not pests.
  • Share results. When a case we handed off leads to an arrest, celebrate it publicly (with LE's permission). This builds trust and shows the system works when we work together.
  • Invite them to speak. If we build a community presence, inviting ICAC to do awareness events with us builds credibility for both sides.
  • Never burn the bridge. Even when they don't act on a case, we stay respectful. That detective who couldn't help us today might be the one who takes our next case seriously.
  • Stay independent. We never take direction from LE during an active decoy operation. We operate on our own, then hand off after. This protects the case from entrapment claims (more on that below).

What If ICAC Says "Stop"?

It could happen. Some departments don't want civilians doing this work in their jurisdiction. If they explicitly ask us to stop operating in a specific area:

  • We listen. Not because we agree, but because antagonizing the one group of cops who actually investigate these crimes is counterproductive.
  • We shift geography. Four states, dozens of jurisdictions. If one town doesn't want us there, we operate in the next one.
  • We document the refusal. If ICAC refuses to act AND tells us to stop, and that predator goes on to hurt a kid — that's a story that writes itself.
  • We stay available. "We respect your request. If you ever want to work with us, we're here."

Entrapment — It Doesn't Apply To Us

This Is Our Biggest Legal Advantage

Entrapment as a legal defense only works against government agents — law enforcement. It does not apply to private citizens. Period. This is consistent across all four states and basically every jurisdiction in the country.

  • NH: RSA 626:5 — explicitly says "law enforcement officials" only
  • ME: 17-A §101(1) — requires government agent inducement
  • VT: Same principle under Vermont case law
  • MA: Commonwealth v. Tracey (2003) — confirmed, requires government inducement

A predator can't walk into court and say "they entrapped me" when "they" is a group of civilians. That defense is dead on arrival.

Keep It Clean Anyway

Even though entrapment doesn't legally apply to us, we still run clean operations because it makes the evidence stronger, it makes prosecutors more willing to pick up cases, and it makes us bulletproof on camera:

  1. Never start the sexual conversation. The predator drives every escalation.
  2. State the decoy's age clearly and early. First few messages. No ambiguity.
  3. Never suggest the meetup first. Let them propose it.
  4. If they get cold feet, let them go. We don't convince reluctant people to show up.
  5. Save the complete, unedited logs. Every message, every timestamp. This proves who was pushing what.
  6. Make them travel to us. When they drive to a meeting location, that's intent they can't talk their way out of.
The Independence Rule

We operate independently from law enforcement during active decoy operations. If we coordinate with police during the sting itself, a defense attorney could argue we were acting as government agents — which would open the door to entrapment claims. The move is simple: do our work independently, then hand off evidence after the fact. We partner with ICAC on the handoff, not the operation.

Confrontation & What Happens If It Gets Physical

We're going to be straight about this. When you confront a grown man who just drove across town to have sex with who he thought was a 13-year-old, emotions run high. On both sides. Things can get physical. We know that. We accept that.

What We Know Going In

  • Simple assault is a misdemeanor in all four states — typically up to 1 year max. NH: RSA 631:2-a. ME: 17-A §207. VT: 13 V.S.A. §1023. MA: MGL c.265 §13A.
  • Serious bodily injury bumps it to a felony — NH up to 15 yrs, ME/VT/MA similar ranges. We're not trying to put anyone in the hospital. But if a punch lands hard, that's the legal landscape.
  • The realistic odds of a predator calling the cops on US? Slim to none. They just got caught trying to meet a child. The last thing they want is more law enforcement involvement. Most just want to disappear.
  • Three of our four states have Stand Your Ground (NH, ME, VT). If the predator swings first, we're legally protected to defend ourselves without retreating. In MA, there's a duty to retreat — we try to walk away first in MA, but we're not getting hurt to satisfy a statute.

But we accept that if someone does press charges, we're looking at a misdemeanor for simple assault. We've weighed that against letting predators feel safe, and we've made our choice.

Smart Confrontation Practices

  • Always in public spaces — parking lots, parks, public sidewalks. Never on private property.
  • Cameras rolling from the start — context is everything. The full video shows they arrived voluntarily.
  • Lead with questions — "What are you doing here? Who are you here to meet? How old did she say she was?" Get their words on camera first.
  • Have security present — their job is protecting the team, not engaging the predator.
  • If they run, let them run. We got what we need. Following is where stalking/harassment claims come from.
  • If they get aggressive first — three states protect us with Stand Your Ground. Defend yourself, use proportional force, disengage when the threat stops.
  • In Massachusetts specifically — we try to de-escalate and step back first. The duty to retreat is real here.

Citizen's Arrest

All four states allow citizen's arrest for felonies. Online solicitation is a felony. We have the legal right. That said, physically detaining someone is where things get complicated fast — false imprisonment claims, excessive force arguments. The better play 90% of the time is to let them leave and hand the evidence to ICAC. They know who the person is. The evidence speaks. If we need to hold someone for police, we do it verbally — "The police are on their way" — not physically.

Decoy Operations

Who We Recruit

  • All decoys are adults (18+). No exceptions.
  • Best fit: people 18-25 who can carry a conversation as a younger teenager through text
  • Survivors who want to turn their experience into action are some of the strongest volunteers we'll find
  • Background check everyone. If a decoy has a record — especially anything involving dishonesty — a defense attorney will use it to tear apart the case.
  • Train them on legal boundaries, evidence preservation, persona consistency, and emotional resilience. This content is heavy.

Profile Photos & Likeness — Real People Only

Why We Don't Use AI Faces

AI-generated photos don't cut it. Predators ask for video calls. They ask for verification photos — "write my name on a piece of paper and take a selfie." One failed verification and the catch is blown. We need real people behind these profiles who can respond to those requests in real time. The operation has to be believable from start to finish.

What We Need From Decoy Photo Volunteers

  • Adult volunteers (18+) who can pass for younger in photos and on camera
  • A written model release form that spells out exactly how their photos will be used — dating profiles, social media, predator catching operations
  • They need to fully understand the implications: their face will be on profiles used to bait predators, and those profiles may become part of evidence or public content
  • They may need to be available for live video calls or photo verification requests from predators to keep the operation convincing
  • Include a clause for revoking consent if they leave the group
  • Their real identity stays completely confidential — we use their photos but never their real name

Hard rules: Never use actual minors' photos. Never use anyone's photos without their written permission and full understanding of how they'll be used.

Evidence — Building Cases That Stick

Evidence is the difference between a predator getting charged and a predator walking free. Everything we do is built around creating an evidence package so clean that prosecutors have no excuse not to pick it up.

What Makes Evidence Strong

What Gets Cases Made

  • Complete, unedited chat logs with timestamps
  • Clear proof the suspect knew the "minor's" age
  • The suspect initiated and escalated sexual content
  • Suspect voluntarily traveled to a meeting location
  • Voluntary statements on camera (not coerced)
  • Evidence collected within recording laws
  • Documented chain of custody with file hashes

What Kills Cases

  • Edited or cherry-picked chat logs
  • Decoy initiated sexual topics
  • Illegal recordings (two-party state violations)
  • Broken chain of custody
  • Statements made under threats or duress
  • Evidence obtained through hacking

Evidence Preservation Protocol

  1. Screen record every decoy conversation — OBS running, full screen capture with timestamps and platform UI visible
  2. Screenshot in real-time — don't rely on platform history, messages get deleted
  3. Export native chat logs from the platform when possible
  4. SHA-256 hash every file immediately after creation — this proves nothing was tampered with
  5. Never edit original files — editing strips metadata. Keep originals untouched.
  6. Triple backup — local drive, encrypted cloud, and a USB drive specifically for law enforcement
  7. Confrontation video: continuous, unedited, multiple angles — body cam + stationary camera. Never stop recording.

The ICAC Evidence Packet

When we hand off to ICAC, this is what they get:

  • Printed chat logs — chronological, timestamped, complete
  • USB drive — original screenshots, screen recordings, chat exports, confrontation video
  • Written timeline of events from first contact to meetup
  • Decoy profile details — name used, age stated, photos used, volunteer release on file
  • Suspect info — usernames, phone numbers, anything they shared
  • SHA-256 hash log proving file integrity
  • Decoy operator's written statement

Why Civilian Evidence Is Admissible

The Fourth Amendment exclusionary rule — which can get evidence thrown out — only applies to government actors. We're civilians. Evidence we gather is admissible in court in all four states, as long as we didn't break any state laws collecting it (like recording violations). The First Circuit, which covers NH, ME, and MA, has upheld this repeatedly. This is one of the reasons civilian operations can be so effective — we don't have the same constitutional constraints as cops.

Build Cases

When a suspect has: (1) acknowledged age, (2) initiated sexual conversation, (3) proposed meeting — we have a viable case.

12

Execute

Set the meet in a public location. Team in position, cameras rolling. Follow the escalation model — ICAC gets the evidence. If they don't act, we handle it ourselves.
